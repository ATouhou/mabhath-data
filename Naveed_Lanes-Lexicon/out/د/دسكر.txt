دسكردَسْكَرَةٌ A building like a قَصْر [q. v.], surrounded
by houses, or chambers, (Lth, Mgh, Msb, K,) and
places of abode for the servants and household,
(TA,) and pertaining to kings: (Lth, Mgh,
Msb:) Heraclius is related, in trads., to have
received the great men of the Greeks in a دسكرة
belonging to him: (TA:) or a building like a
قَصْر, which is surrounded by houses, or chambers,
and in which the vitious, or immoral, (شُطَّار,)
assemble: (Har p. 140:) or houses of the foreigners (أَعَاجِم), in which are wine and instruments
of music or the like: (K:) thought by Az to be
an arabicized word; (Msb;) not genuine Arabic:
(TA:) [from the Persian دَسْكَرَهْ, or دَسْتْكَرَهْ:] pl.
دَسَاكِرُ. (K.) ―
-b2-  Also A Christian's cloister, or
cell; syn. صَوْمَعَةٌ. (AA, K.) ―
-b3-  And A town,
or village; syn. قَرْيَةٌ. (Az, Msb, K.) ―
-b4-  And A
plain, or level, land. (Kz, K.)
